Case Study

Montgomery County Social Services

Services Design for R.E.A.L. Parenting Program

I led both sales and Service Design responsibilities to research and build tools for Montgomery County Social Services (MCSS) Departments that simplified the process in verifying behavior change in their parenting programs, starting with their R.E.A.L. Parenting Program. The business need emerged as the locality, under newly enacted Family First Prevention Services Act, was required to verify behavior change in parenting programs to begin receiving federal funding for those type of programs.

Planning

Stakeholder Mapping:

The project kicked off with establishing expectations on success outcomes, metrics and outlining engagement responsibilities for client members involved. These assets helped the team understand to what degree select MCSS employees could be involved, as well as called out what challenges might ensue based off involvement availability

Project Manager

"When determining behavior change, I need to understand client process as quickly as possible so I can communicate with my Caseworkers on how and where we may need to intervene."

Our team worked primary alongside MCSS' Program Manager to understand the behavior change measurement process. As this was departments first time conducting Human Center Design research, as well as their first time deploying a cloud-based solution into their own organization without state-level involvement, we were very careful about not asking for too much too early.

Caseworkers needs were communicated from their supervisors, Program Managers, as the technology's impact (e.g. available results, process change, culture impact) laid primarily on the Program Managers shoulders.

Reseach

Business Process Mapping:

Once expectations were established, we worked across their organization to understand how behavior change was currently being verified in their parenting programs.

Pain Point Mapping:

Relevaility quickly, we discovered pain points within how information was being shared from client to Caseworker to program Service Provider; and then visualized how those issues could be resolved

Business Process Re-Mapping:

Synthesis

Low-fidelity Wireframes:

A high-level concept car was produced to illustrate our possible solution’s technology. The crafted screens provided clarity and a path forward to realize a method to close the feedback loop between the client, Caseworker and program Service Provider; which was causing the issue in quicikly verifying behavior change in their current parenting programs.

Ideation

Prototyping:

We tested several versions of the application and, once alignment ensued across our team and MCSS, we advanced the concept to include minimal Artificial Intelligence to both close the feedback loop and create better, faster and more efficient organization awareness on client progress without any human-level involvement. In addition, the finalized direction centralized communication into one source to create a paper trail of what was done; and by whom.

Delivery

Sprint Planning:

Once the concept proved viable, we established Sprints to turn the concept into code. The Design team worked a Sprint before for our Dev team, allowing us to design and usability test ideas before Dev was ready to build. During Dev’s Sprint, we worked hand-and-hand; usability testing screens when ready or tweaking designs to not comprise dev feasibility needs and then retesting when dramatic changes emerged.

Usability Testing:

When conducting Usability Testing, we worked with user separate groups to test the script for clarity and then another to validate proposed functionality.

Reflections:

As the pilot program slowly came to an end, our team quickly realized a pivot was needed. The introduced service introduced needs for a new role, an in-house program evaluator, that existed in the program’s Services Providers. In addition, we also found that some Services Providers were better suited for verifying behavior change in their parenting programs and their Caseworkers (a stark difference from the locality’s Caseworker) contained existing responsibilities that alleviated some of the pain points initially discovered. Their business model, as well, allowed for more experimental technology to be introduced; beyond the confines a simple web or mobile-based application.

Too much emphasis was also given to solving the business problem from the Program Managers perspective, instead of the Caseworkers, as the solution should have been crafted explicitly towards their needs; particularly since the Caseworkers proved to be the most impacted by the technology’s introduction. Our team was, unfortunately, unable to secure enough time to conduct deep dives into Caseworker’s experience of verifying behavior change. My hypothesis is, had this been done, we would have discovered the need to pivot sooner from Caseworkers in Social Services Departments to Caseworker in Social Services Providers; particularly those with evidence-based funding models
